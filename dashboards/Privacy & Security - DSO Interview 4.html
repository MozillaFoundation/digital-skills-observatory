Dealing with passwords

This is a very large set of questions, which we've broken up below. There is a very noticeable effect from the intervention training given to the treatment group: they use a different password for their accounts more often, and use a strong password. However, there is no large group difference when it comes to sharing pins or passwords.

We also found some interesting differences in gender. Females are more likely to use the same password, and are more likely to call their password 'Strong', as opposed to men who pick 'Very strong' more often.

Insert paragraph here...

The treatment group avoids using the same password much more often

Out of the 29 respondents who recently created a new account, most pick a password that is different than one they picked previously.

The treatment group does much better in this regard: 73% avoids using the same password, as opposed to 50% in the control group. But please note that we can't be sure if this relation is strong, because it only counts 29 responses.

For the followup questions, there is an interesting difference: 37% of the respondents in the treatment group say they use the same passwords for their newly created account, as opposed to 30% in the control group.

Reasons for using or not using the same password

For the people who use the same password, the reason is simple: it makes it easier to remember.

The people who don't use the same password say that the intervention training taught them, note 'security purposes' and say they don't want to get hacked.

Changing passwords

The difference between the treatment and control group is staggering when it comes to changing passwords. Almost half of the respondents in the treatment group changed their passwords, as opposed to only 8% in the control group.

Men more often say they change their password than women.

A full two thirds of the people in the treatment group mention the DSO intervention training as the motivation for the password change.

Password strength

Another large difference between groups can be seen when the respondents are asked about password strength.

The treatment group thinks a lot better about their passwords, seeing they mention they are 'Strong' or 'Very strong' 75.9% of the time, as opposed to 59.2% in the control group.

There is also an interesting difference in gender for this question: females are much more likely to call their password 'Strong', and males 'Very strong'. Flip the filter on the chart below to see the difference.

Sharing passwords

Most respondents don't share their passwords, and the most popular reason by far were variations on 'Because it's secret / private / confidential'.

For social media, Facebook is listed as most popular. Gmail comes second (although you could argue that's not really a social media account).

People share passwords because they trust the person, or because they want that person to do something for them on with the account.

There was no big difference between the groups and genders for these questions.

With whom do you share your phone?

Almost half of the respondents don't share their phone with anyone. The median of the number of people respondents share their phone with is 1, and the average is 3. The average is a bit higher, because there are 3 outliers who share their phone with 25, 43 and 100 people.

If we take these outliers out of the dataset, respondents share their phones with 2.1 people on average.

Most respondents who share their phone do so weekly or monthly, and mostly to friends & neighbours, siblings, and spouses.

There was no big difference between the groups for these questions.

The danger of sharing a phone

Most of the respondents are confident that other people won't use applications and accounts when using their phone. They list several ways how they make sure other people can't use their accounts, like not sharing their password or pin, creating strong passwords, and using a security pin / pattern / password on their phone.

Most people don't report challenges with sharing their phone, with the other half listing various annoyances, such as getting files deleted, losing memory cards, having accounts checked, and an empty battery.

38% of the respondents don't think there are any benefits with sharing their phone, but a heartwarming 16 percent saying that it's helpful to maintain a friendship.

There was no big difference between the groups or genders for these questions.

Phone sharing and DFS risks

Most respondents are not scared that someone can hack into their mobile money or banking app account. Among common answers why are 'not scared because they don't have the PIN', 'Because I have a strong password/pin', and 'Because I frequently change my password'.

There was no big difference between the groups or genders for these questions.

Multiple users

Only 11 out of the 172 respondents use the 'multiple users' feature on their phone. 8 of those are in the treatment group.

The respondents that use this feature have either 2 or 3 accounts.

There was no big difference between the groups or genders for these questions.
